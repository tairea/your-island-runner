Mission 1.5: Play Your Game

You are no longer just a student learning software. You are a game developer with a finished game. In the next 15 minutes, you are going to experience your creation as a player, look at it with fresh eyes, and then do something that takes real courage: show it to other people.

Every professional game developer goes through this moment - the transition from building to playing, from creator to critic, from private project to public showcase. Some of the most valuable lessons come not from building, but from watching others play what you built. Today, you join that tradition.

Real Talk: Sharing your work can feel scary. What if someone does not like it? What if they find a bug? Here is the truth: every game has bugs at first, and feedback is how games get better. The developers of your favorite games went through this exact process hundreds of times. You are in good company.

Your Mission

Learning Objective: Test your complete mini-course, make adjustments based on playtesting, and share your creation with classmates.

What You Will Accomplish

  • Play through your entire course multiple times
  • Identify what works well and what could be improved
  • Make at least one adjustment based on playtesting
  • Share your screen and let classmates see your game
  • Celebrate completing your first Roblox game!

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Do a Complete Playtest

It is time to play your game like you have never seen it before. Try to forget that you built it.

  1. Save your project (Ctrl + S)
  2. Click Play
  3. Start from the spawn point and play through your entire course
  4. Try to reach the victory platform
  5. Complete the course at least 3 times

Playtester Mindset: While playing, ask yourself: Is this fun? Is it too easy? Too hard? Does anything feel unfair? Would a new player understand what to do? These questions are what professional playtesters ask.

Step 2: Take Mental Notes

As you play, notice these things:

Jump Difficulty

  • Did you make every jump on the first try? (Might be too easy)
  • Did you fail the same jump 5+ times? (Might be too hard)
  • Did the difficulty increase gradually? (Good design!)

Visual Clarity

  • Could you always see where to jump next?
  • Was the danger zone clearly visible and avoidable?
  • Did the victory platform stand out as the goal?

Feel and Flow

  • Did the course feel satisfying to complete?
  • Were there any frustrating moments?
  • Did you want to play again after finishing?

Step 3: Make At Least One Improvement

Based on your playtest, make at least one change. Here are common improvements:

If a jump was too hard:

  • Move the platforms slightly closer together
  • Make the landing platform slightly bigger
  • Lower the height difference between platforms

If it was too easy:

  • Increase the distance between platforms
  • Make landing platforms smaller
  • Add height changes to make jumps trickier

If something was confusing:

  • Make the danger zone larger so it is more visible
  • Change the victory platform to a brighter color
  • Reposition platforms so the path is clearer

Your Turn!

Click Stop, make one improvement to your course, then Play again to test your change. Does it feel better? Worse? Keep iterating until you are happy!

Step 4: Understand the Iteration Loop

What you just did has a name in game development: iteration. It is the most important process in making games:

1 Build

Create something

→
2 Test

Play it yourself

→
3 Observe

Notice what works and what does not

→
4 Improve

Make changes

→
5 Repeat

Go back to step 2

This loop never really ends. Even published games get updates and patches. The best game developers are the ones who are always willing to iterate and improve.

Step 5: Prepare to Share Your Screen

It is time to show your classmates what you built! Here is how to prepare:

  1. Save your project one final time (Ctrl + S)
  2. Make sure you are in Play mode so others can see the game running
  3. Position your character at the start of the course
  4. When your teacher calls on you, share your screen

Presentation Tip: When sharing, give a quick intro: "This is my obstacle course. The goal is to jump across the platforms to reach the green finish line. The red floor is a kill brick - touch it and you restart!" Then play through your course.

Step 6: Share and Learn from Others

Sharing is a two-way street. Here is what to do:

When Showing Your Game:

  • Play through your course while others watch
  • If you fail a jump, that is okay! It shows the game has challenge
  • Point out features you are proud of
  • Be open to feedback - it helps you improve!

When Watching Others:

  • Pay attention to what they did differently than you
  • Notice good ideas you might want to use later
  • Be encouraging! Everyone is learning
  • If you have constructive feedback, share it kindly

Learning from Others: Seeing how your classmates solved the same challenge differently is incredibly valuable. Maybe someone made their kill brick a different shape. Maybe someone's platforms form an interesting pattern. Take mental notes!

Reflection Questions

After sharing, think about these questions:

  1. What was the hardest part of building this game?
  2. What are you most proud of?
  3. What would you do differently next time?
  4. What did you learn from watching your classmates' games?
  5. What feature would you want to add if you had more time?
