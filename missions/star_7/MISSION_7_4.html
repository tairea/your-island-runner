Part 1: Preparing Your Presentation

What Makes a Great Game Showcase

Your presentation should accomplish three things: show what you made, explain why you made the choices you did, and tell the story behind your island. Think of this as a combination of demo, design talk, and storytelling.

Recommended Presentation Outline (3-5 minutes)

1. The Hook (30 seconds)

  • Start with your island's name and theme
  • One sentence that captures what your game is about
  • Example: "I created Volcano Island Runner, where you race across an erupting volcano to reach the rescue helicopter before the island explodes."

2. The Story (30-45 seconds)

  • Share your island's backstory or concept
  • Why did you choose this theme?
  • What atmosphere or feeling did you want players to experience?
  • Example: "I wanted to create a sense of urgency and danger, so I designed an island that's falling apart. The lava rising behind you makes every jump matter."

3. The Demo (1-2 minutes)

  • Screen share or play through the beginning of your course
  • Point out key obstacles and challenges
  • Highlight features you're proud of
  • Show, don't just tell—let them see the game in action
  • Example: "Here's the starting beach, then you jump to these floating rocks, this section has the rotating obstacles I spent two missions perfecting..."

4. Design Highlights (45-60 seconds)

  • Share 2-3 specific design or technical choices you made
  • Explain challenges you faced and how you solved them
  • Mention polish details (lighting, sound, visual effects)
  • Example: "The hardest part was balancing difficulty—I tested the jump distances dozens of times. I also added glowing crystals to guide players toward the path."

5. The Invitation (15-30 seconds)

  • Share your game link (display on screen or in chat)
  • Invite classmates to try it
  • Mention what feedback you'd value
  • Example: "The link is in the chat—I'd love to see how fast you can complete it, and whether the difficulty feels fair!"

Presentation Tips

Effective Delivery

  • Practice your timing: Run through your presentation at least twice to make sure you fit in 3-5 minutes
  • Don't memorize word-for-word: Know your key points and speak naturally
  • Show enthusiasm: Your excitement about your game is contagious
  • Make eye contact: Look at your audience, not just the screen
  • Slow down: Nerves make us rush—deliberately pace yourself
  • Use "I" statements: "I designed..." "I chose..." "I learned..." This is your work—own it!

Handling Demo Technical Issues

  • Have screenshots ready: If live demo fails, show images
  • Pre-load your game: Have it open before you present
  • Test screen sharing: Make sure it works ahead of time
  • Don't panic: If something breaks, explain what should happen and move on
  • Humor helps: "Well, that's live demos for you!" then continue

Visual Support (Optional but Recommended)

While not required, a simple slide or two can enhance your presentation and give the audience something to look at beyond the game itself.

Suggested Slides

  • Title slide: Game name, your name, and your thumbnail image
  • Design highlights: Bullet points of key features or challenges
  • Link slide: Large, readable game URL and/or QR code
  • Thank you slide: Simple ending with your name

Keep slides minimal—big text, few words, striking images. The game itself is your main visual.

Part 2: The Live Demo

Demonstrating Your Game Effectively

The live demo is where your game comes to life for your audience. This is more impactful than any description—they need to see it running.

Demo Best Practices

  • Start from the beginning: Show the spawn experience so viewers understand the complete player journey
  • Narrate as you play: Explain what you're doing and why
    • "Now I'm jumping to this platform..."
    • "This part is tricky because..."
    • "I added this visual effect to show..."
  • Point out polish: Highlight lighting, sounds, effects that viewers might miss
  • Show personality: Celebrate good jumps, react if you fall—be authentic
  • Don't try to "perfect" it: If you die during the demo, that shows the challenge is real
  • Time-conscious: Don't play the entire game—show the first third, then skip to interesting parts

What to Highlight During Demo

  • Your favorite obstacle or section
  • A mechanic you're proud of (moving platforms, collectibles, special jumps)
  • The visual theme and how different sections look
  • Any story elements or environmental storytelling
  • The win condition or ending

Handling Q&A

Your classmates or teacher might ask questions after your presentation. This is a sign of engagement—embrace it!

Common Questions and How to Answer

"How long did it take to make?"

Be honest about time—mention it was built across 7 stars over [X] weeks/days. You can note which parts took longest.

"What was the hardest part?"

Share a specific challenge you faced—maybe balancing difficulty, getting a mechanic to work, or making a design decision.

"Will you add more to it?"

You can discuss future ideas or say you're happy with it as is. Either answer is valid.

"How did you learn to do this?"

Mention the Galaxy Maps curriculum, specific missions that taught key skills, and any experimentation you did.

If you don't know an answer:

It's okay to say "That's a great question—I'm not sure, but I could look into it." Honesty is better than making something up.

Part 3: Playtime and Feedback

Watching Others Play Your Game

One of the most rewarding (and sometimes nerve-wracking) experiences in game development is watching someone else play your creation. You'll learn more from observation than from any amount of testing yourself.

What to Observe

  • Where do they pause or hesitate? This reveals unclear design or confusing navigation
  • Where do they laugh or react? These are your success moments—the parts that work
  • Do they go where you expected? Players often find creative paths or shortcuts
  • Where do they struggle? Note if it's because of skill or because something is unclear
  • Do they seem engaged? Are they focused and trying, or bored and distracted?
  • How long do they play? Do they finish, or give up partway?
Resist the urge to help: Let them figure things out on their own. If they get stuck, that's valuable feedback about your game's clarity, not their skill. Only help if they explicitly ask or if they're completely stuck beyond recovery.

Receiving Feedback Gracefully

Your classmates will share their thoughts on your game. Some feedback will be praise, some will be suggestions, some might be criticism. All of it is valuable if you approach it with the right mindset.

The Professional Feedback Mindset

  • Listen without defending: Don't immediately explain why you made a choice—just hear them out
  • Thank people for feedback: Even if you disagree, appreciate that they took time to play and share thoughts
  • Ask clarifying questions: "What specifically felt too hard?" is more useful than just "too hard"
  • Look for patterns: If three people mention the same issue, it's probably real
  • Separate yourself from your work: Criticism of your game isn't criticism of you
  • Remember what you've accomplished: You made and published a game—that's huge, regardless of specific feedback

Useful Feedback Questions to Ask

  • "What was your favorite part?"
  • "Was anything confusing or unclear?"
  • "Did the difficulty feel fair?"
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how fun was it?"
  • "Would you play more if there were additional levels?"
  • "Any suggestions for improvement?"

Giving Feedback to Others

You'll also play your classmates' games and provide feedback. Be the kind of audience member you'd want for your own presentation.

How to Give Helpful Feedback

  • Start with positives: What did you genuinely enjoy or find impressive?
  • Be specific: "The volcano theme was really cool" is better than "nice job"
  • Frame suggestions constructively: "I'd love to see..." rather than "You should have..."
  • Consider their goals: Evaluate the game based on what they were trying to achieve
  • Balance praise and critique: Aim for 2 positives for every suggestion
  • Avoid comparisons: Don't say "X's game was better" or "This is like Y but worse"
  • Ask about intentions: "Were you going for a hard challenge or casual fun?" before critiquing difficulty
